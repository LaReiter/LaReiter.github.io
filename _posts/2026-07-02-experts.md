## Prediction is hard

Being an expert in sports, the economy or global politics is hard.

This is because such experts are typically expected to predict the future. Or at the very least, assess the impact of some recent event on the future.

If the task was to predict whether an infant would eventually toss his plate of food on the ground, this would have been an easy prediction. But these experts typically have to predict events that involve lots of uncertainties, with many intermediate random events influencing the outcome. 

For example, consider predicting which teams will enter the quarter finals in the ongoing World Cup in soccer. Such predictions are hard. Because even if an expert can rank teams well by their overall strength, they cannot predict the game dynamics, injuries or the mental state of the players.

## Noisy outcomes

When an expert is asked to make a prediction, the expert will use his expertise which is grounded in theoretical considerations and empirical observations.  

The problem with empirical observations, however, is that they are noisy. As already mentioned, there are many intermediate random events that lead up to a given observation. Typically, the expert is not exposed to such events. 

Going back to the soccer example, the expert might have a good idea of the overall strength of the teams, but is unaware of the players' individual state, the quality of the soccer fields, the lenience of the referees and so forth.  
When a high ranked team then proceeds to the quarter final, the expert might look back and perceive the success of the team as quite predictable, due to a selected number of factors, when in fact many hidden random factors turned out to be just as important, and turned out just right. This is called hindsight bias.

If the expert then becomes overconfident and uses this perceived knowledge (the winning factors) to make a future prediction, it might not hold at all. The team might fail, or it might succeed, but for reasons unrelated to whatever factors the expert deemed important. The image above shows this: on the left, an expert who, after a horse race, knew exactly why that particular horse won. Using this perceived knowledge, he then put his money on the same horse for the next race, and was confused when it did not win. 

## Time changes everything

A second, more subtle problem is time. Even a clean observation, free of noise, is tied to the moment it was made, and it might not transfer to a later moment.

The deeper issue is that the world does not stay still while we learn from it. By the time the expert has figured out why something happened, the conditions that made it happen may already have changed.

Returning once again to the soccer example, an expert might provide reasons for why one or the other team succeeds, based on the team's performance in the World Cup qualification.

But between the qualification and the World Cup various things could have influenced the shape of the team, making such observations less evidential.

## The expert

Experts making predictions are without a doubt more trustworthy than a random person on the street doing the same.

But still, expert or not, we should be careful with confident, tidy explanations offered after the fact. A prediction that comes with a clean story is not more reliable for it. If anything, the story hides how much of the outcome was noise.
