## Reality

Unless you are someone who holds that truth is entirely a matter of perspective (e.g. a relativist) you will likely agree that there are rational reasons for why things happen. 

For example, as the stem of an apple weakens, the pull of gravity will eventually exceed the counter forces that are keeping it on the tree branch. As a result, the apple will fall to the ground. This is a causal and factual explanation building on some well supported model of reality. 

Whether this model is completely accurate is beyond the point. What matters is, that everything in this world, whether physical or immaterial, can in principle be traced to some underlying mechanism. 

This mechanism can be complex and contain several interacting components. Take for example, a persons life trajectory. Why did any of us turn out the way we did? A consultant? A mom? A billionaire? Likely we will never be able to give a full answer to such a question, but we might guess that it has several components, including genetics and environmental factors.

## Our worldview

Many of us see the world in a specific set of colors. By that I mean the way we perceive the world passes through a filter that attenuates or amplifies our experiences. The filter is built continuously, and reinforced by our current understanding.

For some, this filter can be due to a creative or imaginative mindset: we want the world to be adventurous, and so we weight those phenomena that strengthen such a worldview. For others, it can be due to a judgemental, principled or otherwise narrow state, that allows only certain phenomena to alter our worldview.

## The distortion

When we are children, our filter is mostly white: all phenomena, irrespective of their color, are weighted equally. A small child will be similarly intrigued by a falling leaf and a yellow Lamborghini, and will be less judgemental than any grown-up.

As the child matures, the filter changes colors. This can be due to genetic predisposition - perhaps we are more inclined towards certain worldviews. But it can also stem from social interactions: when we age, we are painted more and more by the people surrounding us. This is visualized in the Figure below.

![Population Perception Flowchart](/assets/perception_population.png)

A colored filter is problematic, because it implies a distortion of reality. We do not weight the evidence (phenomena) of the world equally, and thus are not neutral observers of reality. Instead, the world is projected differently onto each of us, leading to a mass of opposing worldviews. The strong illusion that the world is as we perceive it or that we are able to provide an objective representation of reality is false. While we may attempt whitening our filter, by giving more weight to evidence opposing our worldview, the filter will likely still distort reality.

In essence, when humans stand between observing the world and understanding the world, biases (e.g. confirmation bias) will pollute our understanding. 

On statistical grounds we might hope, that when people with different sets of filters - hence different sets of biases - all seek to answer the same question, such individual biases can just be interpreted as noise. The aggregation of opinions might then serve as the most objective way to describe reality.
